Theoretical Foundations of LDEs

Core definitions, theorems, and mathematical foundations

First-Order Linear Differential Equations

Standard Form

A first-order linear differential equation has the form:

\( \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x) \)

where \( P(x) \) and \( Q(x) \) are continuous functions of \( x \).

Integrating Factor (\( \mu(x) \))

The integrating factor is defined as:

\( \mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx} \)

Multiplying both sides of the standard form by \( \mu(x) \):

\( \frac{d}{dx}[\mu(x)y] = \mu(x)Q(x) \)

General Solution

The general solution of a first-order LDE is given by:

\( y = \frac{1}{\mu(x)} \left[ \int \mu(x)Q(x) dx + C \right] \)

where \( C \) is the constant of integration.

Second-Order Linear Differential Equations

Standard Form

A second-order linear differential equation with constant coefficients:

\( a \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + b \frac{dy}{dx} + c y = 0 \)

where \( a, b, c \) are constants and \( a \neq 0 \).

Auxiliary (Characteristic) Equation

To solve, we assume a solution of the form \( y = e^{rx} \), leading to:

\( a r^2 + b r + c = 0 \)

This quadratic equation is called the auxiliary or characteristic equation.

Wronskian and Linear Independence

For two solutions \( y_1(x) \) and \( y_2(x) \), the Wronskian is:

\( W(y_1, y_2) = \begin{vmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \\ y_1' & y_2' \end{vmatrix} = y_1 y_2' - y_2 y_1' \)

If \( W(y_1, y_2) \neq 0 \) for some \( x \), then \( y_1 \) and \( y_2 \) are linearly independent and form a fundamental set of solutions.

Root Analysis of Auxiliary Equation

Case 1: Real and Distinct Roots

If \( r_1 \neq r_2 \) and both are real:

\( y = C_1 e^{r_1 x} + C_2 e^{r_2 x} \)

Example: \( r^2 - 3r + 2 = 0 \) gives \( r = 1, 2 \)

Case 2: Real and Repeated Roots

If \( r_1 = r_2 = r \):

\( y = (C_1 + C_2 x)e^{r x} \)

Example: \( r^2 - 4r + 4 = 0 \) gives \( r = 2, 2 \)

Case 3: Complex Conjugate Roots

If \( r = \alpha \pm i\beta \):

\( y = e^{\alpha x}[C_1 \cos(\beta x) + C_2 \sin(\beta x)] \)

Example: \( r^2 + 4 = 0 \) gives \( r = \pm 2i \)

Algorithmic Solution Methods

Step-by-step approaches for solving Linear Differential Equations

Method 1: Solving 1st Order LDEs using Integrating Factor

1

Write in Standard Form

Ensure the equation is in the form:

\( \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x) \)

If not, rearrange terms to match this form.

Example: \( \frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = x^2 \) has \( P(x) = 2 \), \( Q(x) = x^2 \)

2

Compute Integrating Factor

Calculate \( \mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx} \)

\( \mu(x) = e^{\int 2 dx} = e^{2x} \)

Don't include the constant of integration when computing \( \mu(x) \).

3

Multiply Equation by \( \mu(x) \)

Multiply both sides of the standard form by the integrating factor:

\( e^{2x} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2e^{2x} y = x^2 e^{2x} \)

Notice the left side is now \( \frac{d}{dx}[e^{2x}y] \)

4

Integrate Both Sides

Integrate with respect to \( x \):

\( \frac{d}{dx}[e^{2x}y] = x^2 e^{2x} \)
\( e^{2x}y = \int x^2 e^{2x} dx + C \)
5

Solve for \( y \)

Divide by \( \mu(x) \) to obtain the general solution:

\( y = e^{-2x} \left[ \int x^2 e^{2x} dx + C \right] \)

Evaluate the integral and simplify.

Method 2: Solving 2nd Order Homogeneous LDEs

1

Write Homogeneous Equation

Ensure the equation is in the form:

\( a y'' + b y' + c y = 0 \)

where \( a, b, c \) are constants, \( a \neq 0 \).

Example: \( y'' - 3y' + 2y = 0 \)

2

Form Characteristic Equation

Assume \( y = e^{rx} \) and substitute to get:

\( a r^2 + b r + c = 0 \)

For our example: \( r^2 - 3r + 2 = 0 \)

3

Solve Characteristic Equation

Find the roots using quadratic formula:

\( r = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \)

For our example: \( r = \frac{3 \pm \sqrt{9 - 8}}{2} = \frac{3 \pm 1}{2} \)

So \( r_1 = 2 \), \( r_2 = 1 \)

4

Write General Solution Based on Roots

Case A: Real Distinct Roots

If \( r_1 \neq r_2 \) (real):

\( y = C_1 e^{r_1 x} + C_2 e^{r_2 x} \)

Our example: \( y = C_1 e^{2x} + C_2 e^{x} \)

Case B: Real Repeated Roots

If \( r_1 = r_2 = r \):

\( y = (C_1 + C_2 x)e^{r x} \)

Example: For \( r^2 - 4r + 4 = 0 \), \( y = (C_1 + C_2 x)e^{2x} \)

Case C: Complex Roots

If \( r = \alpha \pm i\beta \):

\( y = e^{\alpha x}[C_1 \cos(\beta x) + C_2 \sin(\beta x)] \)

Example: For \( r^2 + 4 = 0 \), \( y = C_1 \cos(2x) + C_2 \sin(2x) \)

5

Apply Initial Conditions (if given)

Use conditions like \( y(x_0) = y_0 \), \( y'(x_0) = y_0' \) to find \( C_1 \) and \( C_2 \).

Example: If \( y(0) = 1 \) and \( y'(0) = 0 \), substitute to solve for constants.

Method 3: Method of Undetermined Coefficients

For non-homogeneous equations: \( a y'' + b y' + c y = F(x) \)

1

Find Complementary Solution (\( y_c \))

Solve the homogeneous equation \( a y'' + b y' + c y = 0 \) to get \( y_c \).

Example: For \( y'' - 3y' + 2y = e^{3x} \), first solve \( y'' - 3y' + 2y = 0 \)

Characteristic equation: \( r^2 - 3r + 2 = 0 \) gives \( r = 1, 2 \)

So \( y_c = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{2x} \)

2

Choose Trial Particular Solution (\( y_p \))

Based on the form of \( F(x) \), choose an appropriate trial solution:

\( F(x) \) Form Trial \( y_p \)
\( e^{kx} \) \( Ae^{kx} \)
\( \sin(kx) \) or \( \cos(kx) \) \( A\sin(kx) + B\cos(kx) \)
Polynomial of degree \( n \) \( A_n x^n + A_{n-1} x^{n-1} + ... + A_0 \)
\( e^{kx} \times \) (polynomial) \( e^{kx} \times \) (polynomial of same degree)

Our example: \( F(x) = e^{3x} \), so try \( y_p = Ae^{3x} \)

3

Substitute \( y_p \) into Original Equation

Compute derivatives of \( y_p \) and substitute into the non-homogeneous equation:

For \( y_p = Ae^{3x} \): \( y_p' = 3Ae^{3x} \), \( y_p'' = 9Ae^{3x} \)
Substitute: \( 9Ae^{3x} - 3(3Ae^{3x}) + 2(Ae^{3x}) = e^{3x} \)
Simplify: \( (9A - 9A + 2A)e^{3x} = e^{3x} \)
So: \( 2Ae^{3x} = e^{3x} \)
4

Solve for Undetermined Coefficients

Equate coefficients to solve for the constants in \( y_p \):

\( 2A = 1 \) â‡’ \( A = \frac{1}{2} \)

Thus \( y_p = \frac{1}{2} e^{3x} \)

5

Write General Solution

The general solution is \( y = y_c + y_p \):

\( y = C_1 e^{x} + C_2 e^{2x} + \frac{1}{2} e^{3x} \)

Apply initial conditions if provided to determine \( C_1 \) and \( C_2 \).

Practice Problems

Test your understanding with these LDE problems

Question 1: First-Order LDE

Solve the differential equation: \( \frac{dy}{dx} + 2xy = x \)

Hint:

1. Identify \( P(x) = 2x \) and \( Q(x) = x \)

2. Compute integrating factor: \( \mu(x) = e^{\int 2x dx} = e^{x^2} \)

3. Multiply both sides by \( \mu(x) \): \( e^{x^2} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2x e^{x^2} y = x e^{x^2} \)

4. Recognize left side as \( \frac{d}{dx}[e^{x^2} y] \)

5. Integrate: \( e^{x^2} y = \int x e^{x^2} dx + C \)

Question 2: Homogeneous 2nd Order LDE

Solve: \( \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - 4\frac{dy}{dx} + 4y = 0 \)

Hint:

1. Write characteristic equation: \( r^2 - 4r + 4 = 0 \)

2. Solve: \( (r - 2)^2 = 0 \), so \( r = 2, 2 \) (repeated roots)

3. For repeated roots \( r_1 = r_2 = r \), solution is: \( y = (C_1 + C_2 x)e^{rx} \)

4. Substitute \( r = 2 \): \( y = (C_1 + C_2 x)e^{2x} \)

Question 3: Complex Roots Case

Solve: \( \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 9y = 0 \)

Hint:

1. Characteristic equation: \( r^2 + 9 = 0 \)

2. Roots: \( r = \pm 3i \) (complex conjugates)

3. For \( r = \alpha \pm i\beta \), solution is: \( y = e^{\alpha x}[C_1 \cos(\beta x) + C_2 \sin(\beta x)] \)

4. Here \( \alpha = 0 \), \( \beta = 3 \), so: \( y = C_1 \cos(3x) + C_2 \sin(3x) \)

Question 4: Method of Undetermined Coefficients

Solve: \( y'' - 2y' + y = 2e^{x} \)

Hint:

1. Complementary solution: Solve \( y'' - 2y' + y = 0 \)

2. Characteristic: \( r^2 - 2r + 1 = 0 \) â‡’ \( (r-1)^2 = 0 \) â‡’ \( r = 1, 1 \)

3. So \( y_c = (C_1 + C_2 x)e^{x} \)

4. For particular solution: Since \( e^{x} \) is already in \( y_c \), try \( y_p = Ax^2 e^{x} \)

5. Differentiate, substitute, and solve for \( A \)

Question 5: Initial Value Problem

Solve: \( y'' + 4y = 0 \), with \( y(0) = 1 \), \( y'(0) = 2 \)

Hint:

1. Characteristic: \( r^2 + 4 = 0 \) â‡’ \( r = \pm 2i \)

2. General solution: \( y = C_1 \cos(2x) + C_2 \sin(2x) \)

3. Apply \( y(0) = 1 \): \( C_1 \cos(0) + C_2 \sin(0) = 1 \) â‡’ \( C_1 = 1 \)

4. Differentiate: \( y' = -2C_1 \sin(2x) + 2C_2 \cos(2x) \)

5. Apply \( y'(0) = 2 \): \( -2(1)\sin(0) + 2C_2 \cos(0) = 2 \) â‡’ \( 2C_2 = 2 \) â‡’ \( C_2 = 1 \)

Question 6: RLC Circuit Application

For a series RLC circuit with \( L = 1H \), \( R = 10\Omega \), \( C = 0.01F \), and \( V(t) = 0 \), find the current \( i(t) \) if \( i(0) = 0 \), \( i'(0) = 1 \).

Hint:

1. Circuit equation: \( L\frac{d^2i}{dt^2} + R\frac{di}{dt} + \frac{1}{C}i = \frac{dV}{dt} \)

2. With given values: \( \frac{d^2i}{dt^2} + 10\frac{di}{dt} + 100i = 0 \)

3. Characteristic: \( r^2 + 10r + 100 = 0 \)

4. Roots: \( r = -5 \pm 5\sqrt{3}i \) (underdamped case)

5. Solution: \( i(t) = e^{-5t}[C_1 \cos(5\sqrt{3}t) + C_2 \sin(5\sqrt{3}t)] \)

Question 7: Mass-Spring System

A mass of 2kg is attached to a spring with constant 8N/m. The system is damped with damping constant 4NÂ·s/m. If the mass is displaced 0.5m and released, find the equation of motion.

Hint:

1. Equation: \( m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + c\frac{dx}{dt} + kx = 0 \)

2. Substituting: \( 2\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + 4\frac{dx}{dt} + 8x = 0 \)

3. Simplify: \( \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + 2\frac{dx}{dt} + 4x = 0 \)

4. Characteristic: \( r^2 + 2r + 4 = 0 \)

5. Roots: \( r = -1 \pm \sqrt{3}i \) (underdamped)

6. Initial conditions: \( x(0) = 0.5 \), \( x'(0) = 0 \) (released from rest)

RLC Electrical Circuits

Series RLC Circuit Analysis

Series RLC Circuit

V(t) â†’ R â†’ L â†’ C

Voltage Source â†’ Resistor â†’ Inductor â†’ Capacitor

The governing differential equation for a series RLC circuit is:

\( L\frac{d^2i}{dt^2} + R\frac{di}{dt} + \frac{1}{C}i = \frac{dV}{dt} \)

where:

  • \( L \) = inductance (Henries)
  • \( R \) = resistance (Ohms)
  • \( C \) = capacitance (Farads)
  • \( i \) = current (Amperes)
  • \( V \) = voltage source (Volts)

Solution Approach:

1. Solve the homogeneous equation for natural response

2. Find particular solution for forced response (if \( V(t) \neq 0 \))

3. Apply initial conditions: \( i(0) \) and \( i'(0) \)

Damping Cases:

Overdamped (\( R^2 > 4L/C \))

Two distinct real roots

Slow, non-oscillatory decay

\( i(t) = C_1 e^{r_1 t} + C_2 e^{r_2 t} \)

Critically Damped (\( R^2 = 4L/C \))

Repeated real roots

Fastest return to equilibrium without oscillation

\( i(t) = (C_1 + C_2 t)e^{rt} \)

Underdamped (\( R^2 < 4L/C \))

Complex conjugate roots

Oscillatory decay

\( i(t) = e^{\alpha t}[C_1 \cos(\beta t) + C_2 \sin(\beta t)] \)

Practical Example: RLC Circuit Design

Design Problem: Design an RLC filter with cutoff frequency 1kHz.

Given: \( L = 10mH \), required \( Q \)-factor = 5

Find: \( R \) and \( C \) values

\( \omega_0 = 2\pi \times 1000 = 2000\pi \, \text{rad/s} \)
\( \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} \Rightarrow C = \frac{1}{L\omega_0^2} \)
\( Q = \frac{1}{R}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} \Rightarrow R = \frac{1}{Q}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} \)

Mechanical Vibration Analysis

Mass-Spring-Damper System

Wall â€” Spring (k) â€” Mass (m) â€” Damper (c)

Displacement: \( x(t) \) from equilibrium

The equation of motion for a damped harmonic oscillator:

\( m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + c\frac{dx}{dt} + kx = F(t) \)

where:

  • \( m \) = mass (kg)
  • \( c \) = damping coefficient (NÂ·s/m)
  • \( k \) = spring constant (N/m)
  • \( x \) = displacement from equilibrium (m)
  • \( F(t) \) = external force (N)

Engineering Significance:

  • Vehicle Suspension: Design for comfort and stability
  • Building Design: Earthquake-resistant structures
  • Machinery: Vibration analysis in rotating equipment
  • Aerospace: Control surface flutter prevention

Damping Ratio (\( \zeta \)) Analysis:

Underdamped (\( \zeta < 1 \))

Oscillatory motion with exponential decay

\( x(t) = e^{-\zeta\omega_n t}[A\cos(\omega_d t) + B\sin(\omega_d t)] \)

where \( \omega_d = \omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2} \)

Critically Damped (\( \zeta = 1 \))

Fastest return to equilibrium without oscillation

\( x(t) = (A + Bt)e^{-\omega_n t} \)

Optimal for many engineering applications

Overdamped (\( \zeta > 1 \))

Slow, non-oscillatory return to equilibrium

\( x(t) = Ae^{r_1 t} + Be^{r_2 t} \)

where \( r_{1,2} = -\zeta\omega_n \pm \omega_n\sqrt{\zeta^2-1} \)

Practical Example: Car Suspension Design

Design Problem: Design suspension for a car with mass 1000kg per wheel.

Requirements: Natural frequency 1.5Hz, damping ratio 0.7

Find: Spring constant \( k \) and damping coefficient \( c \)

\( \omega_n = 2\pi \times 1.5 = 3\pi \, \text{rad/s} \)
\( \omega_n = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \Rightarrow k = m\omega_n^2 \)
\( \zeta = \frac{c}{2\sqrt{km}} \Rightarrow c = 2\zeta\sqrt{km} \)

Electrical-Mechanical Analogy

Electrical System Mechanical System Relationship
Voltage \( V \) Force \( F \) Driving function
Current \( i \) Velocity \( v \) Rate variable
Charge \( q \) Displacement \( x \) Integral of rate
Inductance \( L \) Mass \( m \) \( L\frac{di}{dt} \) â†” \( m\frac{dv}{dt} \)
